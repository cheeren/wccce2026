What's the difference?

  • Greedy Minimax: At each node, pick the guess that minimizes the largest remaining partition. Fast to compute, but only looks one level ahead.
  • True Minimax: Exhaustively try all possible guesses and recursively find the tree with minimum worst-case depth. Guarantees optimal worst case.
  • Min Average: Pick the guess that minimizes expected remaining candidates (weighted by probability). Optimizes for the average case, not worst case.
  • Information Gain (ID3): Pick the guess that maximizes information gain — the reduction in entropy. This is what classic ML decision tree algorithms (ID3, C4.5) use. Entropy H = -Σ pᵢ log₂(pᵢ).
  • Worst Strategy: Deliberately picks guesses that don't discriminate well (words with common letter O). Shows what happens with bad strategy.

Lower Bound: How good can we possibly be?

Each guess produces one of 3 branching outcomes (Jotto scores 0, 1, or 2). Getting a score of 3 means you guessed correctly — game over! That's not a branch, it's a win.

Information-theoretic lower bound: A perfect 3-way split at each level gives us 3d leaves at depth d. To identify 16 words, we need at least:

3d ≥ 16  ⟹  d ≥ log3(16) ≈ 2.52  ⟹  d ≥ 3

So theoretically, we need at least 3 guesses in the worst case. Can we achieve this? That would require our first guess to split the 16 words into 3 groups of roughly equal size, and each follow-up to continue splitting evenly. Looking at the score matrix... no sequence of guesses achieves this!

The best we can do is depth 6 — twice the theoretical minimum. This gap shows that our word set has "entanglement" — words share letters in ways that prevent perfect discrimination.

Computational Complexity

Finding the optimal decision tree (minimum depth or size) is NP-hard! This was proven by Hyafil & Rivest in 1976. Even approximating the minimum depth within a constant factor is hard.

That's why real-world ML systems (ID3, C4.5, CART, random forests) use greedy heuristics like information gain or Gini impurity — they're fast and usually produce good (if not optimal) trees.

For our 16-word Jotto, exhaustive search is feasible. But for Wordle's 2,309 words? The search space explodes, and greedy methods become essential.
